Hazel and the Hundred-Acre Hush

  • Ages 4-8
  • 9 min read
  • Moral: Small duties done with love
    • 10-Minute Bedtime StoriesAnimal Bedtime StoriesBedtime Stories About Kindness

In the middle of the Hundred-Acre Wood, in a hollow oak with a red door, was the smallest post office in the world. It had one stamp, one desk, one lamp — and one postmaster: a small brown rabbit named Hazel.

Hazel took her job very seriously. Letters between squirrels. Packages for badgers. Birthday cards that absolutely had to arrive by teatime. All day she hopped the winding paths of the wood with her leather satchel, and by evening her paws ached in the particular, satisfied way of paws that have done their work.

She was locking the red door, ready for her own warm burrow, when she saw it: one last parcel, sitting in the middle of the desk.

It was small and round and wrapped in brown paper, tied with a blade of sweet grass. And on the label, in handwriting she didn’t recognize — soft, silvery handwriting, like someone had written with moonlight — it said:

To: Everyone. Tonight, before sleep.

Hazel looked out the window. The sun was already low and honey-colored, and the wood was beginning its long exhale into evening.

“Everyone,” she sighed, “is a great many stops.”

But a parcel is a parcel, and a postmaster is a postmaster. She slung the satchel over her shoulder and set out.

Her first stop was the beech tree where the squirrel family lived. They were having their usual bedtime argument about whether acorns needed counting one more time before sleep (the little ones voted yes; their mother voted absolutely not). Hazel held up the parcel. “Delivery for Everyone.”

The squirrels gathered round, whiskers twitching. And a curious thing happened: the moment the little ones touched the brown paper, they yawned — all four at once, in perfect harmony, like a tiny choir. The acorns suddenly seemed thoroughly counted. The argument dissolved into blinking and snuggling.

“Well,” said their mother, wondering and grateful. “Whatever it is, we’ve received it.”

Hazel hopped on, though she could not for the life of her say what had been delivered. The parcel was still in her satchel, unopened, exactly as round and exactly as mysterious as before.

At the riverbank, the otter pups were refusing to get out of the water — their favorite bedtime protest. “Delivery for Everyone,” called Hazel, and held up the parcel. The pups paddled over, dripping and curious. One sniff of the sweet-grass string and their splashing turned to slow floating, then to drowsy drifting, until their mother could scoop them up one by one, heavy and warm as loaves.

It happened everywhere. The badger cubs stopped wrestling mid-tumble and folded down into a heap of striped fur. The wren chicks quit teetering on the nest edge and tucked themselves under their mother’s wing without a single peep of protest. Old Mr. Hedgehog, who claimed he hadn’t slept properly since autumn, took one look at the parcel and dozed off leaning on his own gate, smiling.

And Hazel hopped on, stop after stop, while the wood grew quieter and quieter around her — a hush spreading behind her pawprints like ripples behind a boat. By the time she reached the far edge of the wood, the whole forest had gone soft and sleeping, and the first stars were out, and the only one still awake in the entire Hundred-Acre Wood was one small rabbit with one undelivered parcel.

Because that was the puzzle, wasn’t it? She had delivered it everywhere. And it was still in her satchel.

Hazel sat down on a mossy stone to think. Her ears drooped with tiredness. She turned the little parcel over in her paws, and looked again at the silvery label.

To: Everyone.

“…and I suppose,” she said slowly, “that Everyone includes me.”

She untied the sweet-grass string. The brown paper unfolded, soft as a moth’s wing.

Inside was — nothing.

Or rather: inside was the thing you cannot see, only feel. A hush. The deep, plush, velvet quiet of a whole wood safely asleep. It rose out of the paper and settled over her shoulders like the softest blanket ever made, and Hazel understood at last what she had been carrying all evening.

Rest. Somebody, somewhere — perhaps the Moon; the handwriting did rather look like hers — had mailed the forest its goodnight. And it had needed a postmaster faithful enough to carry it to every last door, even with aching paws, even past her own bedtime.

Hazel yawned the greatest yawn of her small life.

She didn’t quite remember hopping home. Home may, in fact, have come to her — the wood shifting kindly in the dark to shorten the path, which is a thing words can’t prove but rabbits know. She curled into her burrow, nose under paws, satchel hung on its hook, empty at last.

And the Hundred-Acre Wood slept, every single creature in it, under one wide quilt of stars.

Last delivery of the day, little one. It’s addressed to you, too.

The end. 🐰
