The Great Blanket Balloon Voyage

  • Ages 4-8
  • 9 min read
  • Moral: Wonder
    • 10-Minute Bedtime StoriesAdventure Bedtime StoriesOcean Bedtime Stories

Nora and Ben were supposed to be asleep.

They were in bed, which their father said was “most of the way there.” The lamp was off. The blanket was pulled up. But their eyes were open in the dark, and Ben whispered the words that always started trouble:

“I’m not even a little bit sleepy.”

“Me neither,” whispered Nora. “Not one crumb.”

And it was exactly then — as if the night had been waiting for someone to say it — that the blanket began to fill with air.

It rose slowly above them, rounder and rounder, swelling up like bread dough, like a sail, like… “A balloon,” breathed Nora. The corners of the bedsheet knotted themselves neatly to the four bedposts. The mattress creaked, wiggled, and lifted gently off the floor.

The window swung open on its own, wide and welcoming.

And the bed — blanket ballooning above, pillows tucked snug at the rail — sailed out into the enormous, star-sprinkled night.

They rose over their own street first. It was strange and wonderful to see it from above: the rooftops like a row of sleeping turtles, the streetlamps making little pools of gold, Mr. Alvarez’s cat trotting home along a fence with somewhere important to be.

“Everyone’s asleep,” whispered Ben.

“Not everyone,” said Nora. “Look.”

The night, it turned out, was busy. Not loud-busy like daytime — gentle-busy. The bed drifted over a bakery where the baker was already dusting flour for tomorrow’s bread, and the warm smell rose up to meet them like a hug. It floated past a hospital where a nurse in a quiet ward rocked a brand-new baby who was too excited about being alive to sleep. Down at the harbor, a fishing boat slipped out over black glass water, its little green lamp winking.

The bed sailed on, out past the edge of town, and the world below turned to silver-dark sea.

“Whales sleep floating,” Nora whispered — she had read it in a book. “Straight up and down, like they’re standing.”

And there they were. The bed drifted low over a pod of great grey whales, dozing upright beneath the surface like a grove of gentle trees, moonlight rippling over their backs. One of them, dreaming, let out a long slow breath — whoooosh — and the warm mist of it nudged the blanket balloon higher, the way you’d boost a child over a wall.

“Thank you,” Ben called down softly. The whale, still asleep, seemed to smile. Whales usually do.

They crossed the sea and came to the far shore, where a city of a thousand lights was tucking itself in — windows going dark in twos and threes, like the city was playing a very slow game of blinking. They floated over mountains where snow glowed blue in the starlight, and a line of sleepy goats stood dreaming on a ledge, and the wind hummed over the peaks like a finger circling the rim of a glass.

Up here, the air was full of small travelers: night birds heading home, migrating swans in a long V that parted politely around the bed and re-joined on the other side, and once — Ben swore it forever after — a boy on a flying carpet who waved as he passed and called something friendly in a language made of chimes.

Nora yawned first. It was the goats that did it; watching anything dream on a ledge is powerful business.

“I’m not sleepy,” she said, yawning enormously.

“Me neither,” said Ben, whose eyes were closed.

The blanket balloon knew, the way beds know. It banked in a long, easy circle and began the drift home — back over the humming mountains, back across the whale-grove sea (all still standing, all still smiling), back past the fishing boat now hauling in silver nets, back over the bakery where the first loaves were going golden.

The sky at the edge of the world was turning the color of peaches.

Their own street rose to meet them, soft and familiar. The window opened. The bed floated in and settled onto the floor with the smallest bump, like a period at the end of a very long, very good sentence. The blanket sighed out its air and lay down flat and warm across them, tucking its own corners in.

When their father came in the next morning, sunlight was everywhere and the two of them were sleeping like stones.

“Up, up, sleepyheads. Did you sleep well?”

Nora blinked. Ben stretched. They looked at each other.

“We went everywhere,” said Ben.

“We saw the whales standing up,” said Nora.

“Of course you did,” said their father, in the voice grown-ups use when they think something is imaginary — but as he gathered up the blanket, a single white feather drifted out of a fold and rocked slowly down to the floor.

A swan’s feather.

He looked at it for a long moment. Then he tucked it in his pocket, said nothing, and went down to make pancakes.

Because here is a secret about grown-ups: once upon a time, on some long-ago night when they weren’t even a little bit sleepy…

…their beds flew too.

The end. 🎈
